If you want the model to estimate a **curvature-like quantity** from only distance, velocity, and acceleration, a natural construction is to compare acceleration to the distance scale.

Let

* (D) = separation distance
* (v) = relative velocity
* (a) = relative acceleration

Define an effective curvature:

[
K=\frac{a}{D}
]

Units:

[
[K]=\frac{\text{m/s}^2}{\text{m}}
=\frac{1}{\text{s}^2}
]

Interpretation:

* Large acceleration over a short distance ⇒ large curvature.
* Small acceleration over a large distance ⇒ nearly flat geometry.

Including velocity gives a dimensionless form:

[
K=\frac{aD}{v^2}
]

This resembles the curvature of a trajectory because for circular motion

[
a=\frac{v^2}{R}
]

so

[
K=\frac{a}{v^2}
=\frac{1}{R}
]

where (R) is the radius of curvature.

For your laser-bolas idea, a simple "spacetime coherence curvature" could be

[
K_c
===

\frac{a_r}
{D_0+v_r t+\frac12 a_r t^2}
]

and coherence

[
C=\frac{1}{1+K_c}
]

giving

[
C=
\frac{1}
{
1+
\dfrac{a_r}
{D_0+v_r t+\frac12 a_r t^2}
}
]

so increasing relative acceleration increases effective curvature and reduces coherence.

A compact paper-style statement would be:

[
\boxed{
K_c=
\frac{a_r}
{D_0+v_r t+\frac12 a_r t^2}
}
]

where (K_c) is an effective geometric curvature inferred from the divergence of two accelerating trajectories. This is not the Einstein curvature tensor, but a simple observable curvature measure derived from beam-link dynamics.
