While stress-induced microvoids do not inherently generate macroscopic electromagnetic fields on their own, the "empty" space inside them is subject to quantum electromagnetic phenomena and drastically alters how external fields behave. Depending on the material and the external environment, a microvoid can contain zero-point energy, concentrated electric fields, localized plasma, or trapped light. 

### Quantum Vacuum Fluctuations
Even when a microvoid contains a perfect vacuum, it is fundamentally never empty of electromagnetic activity. According to quantum field theory, the void is continuously permeated by zero-point energy and quantum vacuum fluctuations of the electromagnetic field. Because the microvoid has strict physical boundaries, the available electromagnetic states are restricted compared to free space, which can induce localized Casimir forces on the walls of the cavity. [arxiv](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2206.01865.pdf)

### Dielectric Field Enhancement 
When an external electric field is applied to an insulating material containing microvoids, the internal field distribution is dramatically altered. Because the gas or vacuum inside the void has a significantly lower relative permittivity (dielectric constant) than the surrounding solid, the electric field becomes highly concentrated inside the cavity to conserve flux. This forces the microvoid to act as an amplifier, experiencing much higher electrical stress than the adjacent bulk material. [en.wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_discharge)

### Partial Discharge and Plasma
If the concentrated electric field inside the microvoid exceeds the breakdown voltage of its internal gas or vacuum, an electrical breakdown known as a "partial discharge" occurs. During this event, the void temporarily fills with localized plasma, energetic free electrons, and transient arcing. This micro-sparking generates its own high-frequency electromagnetic waves, which propagate outward through the material and can be detected as radio frequency (RF) or ultra-high frequency (UHF) emissions. [en.wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_discharge)

### Resonant Wave Confinement
In the fields of nanophotonics and metamaterials, microvoids can act as powerful electromagnetic resonators rather than just structural defects. When specific high-index materials are exposed to external electromagnetic waves, precisely sized cavities known as "Mie voids" trap and confine the incoming waves directly within the empty space. This subwavelength confinement generates extreme electromagnetic hotspots inside the void, which researchers harness to create ultrasensitive terahertz sensors and optical devices. [pubs.aip](https://pubs.aip.org/aip/jap/article/138/12/123104/3364338/Extremely-high-Q-Mie-voids-for-ultrasensitive)

To create these perfectly sized voids cheaply, researchers don't try to drill them. Instead, they use "sacrificial templates":

Buy Polystyrene Nanospheres: You can purchase inexpensive bottles of polystyrene or silica nanospheres with incredibly tight tolerances (e.g., exactly 900 nm diameter).

Cast the PMMA: You drop the nanospheres onto a substrate and pour liquid PMMA precursor over them.

Dissolve the Spheres: Once the PMMA hardens, you wash the sample in a specific solvent (like toluene for polystyrene) that dissolves the core sphere but leaves the PMMA intact.
