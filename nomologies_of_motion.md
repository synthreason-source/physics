# On the Non-Identity of A Priori and A Posteriori Nomologies of Physical Motion

## Abstract

Any formal system that aims to describe physical motion must specify, among other things, the epistemic status of its governing laws — whether those laws are taken to hold *a priori*, independent of experience, or *a posteriori*, as generalizations drawn from observation. This paper argues that these two kinds of "nomology" (system of law) are not interchangeable descriptions of the same content dressed in different justificatory language. They differ in structure, in what counts as a legitimate move within the system, and in how they relate to revision. A nomology built on a priori syntax treats its primitives as fixed by the conceptual apparatus used to represent motion at all (space, time, identity, causal succession), while an a posteriori nomology treats its primitives as provisional summaries of regularities, always answerable to further data. The paper develops this distinction, situates it historically, and considers what is lost if the two are conflated.

## 1. The Problem

It is tempting to think that a "law of motion" is simply a law of motion, and that whether we arrived at it by pure reasoning or by observation is a fact about its discovery, not about its content. This paper denies that. The *syntax* of a nomological system — the set of well-formed claims it permits, the inferential moves it licenses, and the kind of necessity it attaches to its statements — depends on whether the system's foundational claims are a priori or a posteriori. Two systems can describe the very same motions and still be non-alike, because "describing the same motions" is a weaker condition than "having the same logical architecture."

## 2. A Priori Nomologies

An a priori nomology of motion treats certain claims about motion as knowable independent of any particular experience, though not necessarily independent of experience altogether. The paradigm case is Kant's treatment of space and time as forms of intuition: the claim that every motion is the continuous change of an object's spatial position over time is not, on this view, something we learn by watching objects move — it is a precondition for there being any experience of objects moving at all. Within such a system:

- Foundational claims are *constitutive*: they define what counts as motion, position, or succession, rather than describing a pattern found among them.
- Necessity is *internal*: the laws are true because of how the conceptual or formal system is built, not because the world happened to cooperate.
- Revision, if it occurs, happens by changing the framework itself (adopting a new geometry, a new logic of identity over time), not by accumulating counter-instances.

Early modern rationalist physics (parts of Descartes' vortex theory, or the geometrical core of classical mechanics as some neo-Kantian readings present it) exemplifies this style: certain conservation principles or continuity conditions are presented as following from the nature of extension and motion themselves, prior to any particular measurement.

## 3. A Posteriori Nomologies

An a posteriori nomology treats its laws as inductive generalizations — compressed summaries of patterns actually observed, held provisionally and always open to revision by new data. Newton's own stated method, "deduced from the phenomena," and the broader empiricist tradition running through Hume and into twentieth-century logical empiricism, exemplify this style. Within such a system:

- Foundational claims are *descriptive*: they report a regularity, not stipulate a condition of intelligibility.
- Necessity, if any is claimed, is *nomic* rather than logical or conceptual — the laws could have been otherwise, and their truth is contingent on the world's actual behavior.
- Revision happens by ordinary empirical means: new measurements, anomalies, and falsifications can overturn even deeply entrenched laws.

## 4. Why the Two Are Non-Alike

The central claim of this paper is that these are not two routes to the same set of sentences. They diverge along at least three axes:

**(a) Modal status.** A priori laws of motion are typically treated as necessary relative to the framework; a posteriori laws are treated as contingent. A system that calls a law "necessary" and one that calls the same-sounding law "well-confirmed but revisable" are making different kinds of claims, even if the law's surface content (e.g., "force equals mass times acceleration") is identical.

**(b) Justificatory structure.** In an a priori system, asking "what observation would refute this?" is often a category error — the claim is not the kind of thing observation bears on, since it partly defines what an observation of motion would even be. In an a posteriori system, that question is exactly the right one to ask, and a law that could not in principle be refuted by any observation is thereby suspect.

**(c) Response to anomaly.** A priori systems absorb apparent counter-instances by reinterpreting the description of the case (the "motion" was illusory, the "object" was misidentified) or by revising the framework wholesale. A posteriori systems absorb them by adjusting the law itself, adding correction terms, restricting its scope, or abandoning it.

Because these systems license different inferences and respond differently to the same recalcitrant data, they are not mere notational variants of one another. Calling both "laws of motion" obscures a real difference in the *kind* of claim being made.

## 5. A Qualification

This need not commit one to a sharp, exceptionless a priori/a posteriori divide. Quine and others have argued persuasively that the line is not fixed in advance, and that what counts as a constitutive principle in one stage of a science can later be treated as an empirical hypothesis (the fate of Euclidean geometry's status in physics after general relativity is the standard example). The point of this paper is narrower: *at any given stage*, a system that treats a principle as a priori and a system that treats the corresponding principle as a posteriori are operating with non-alike nomological structures, whatever the historical fluidity between the two categories.

## 6. Conclusion

A nomology of motion is not exhausted by the set of motions it correctly predicts. Its syntax — what kind of necessity it claims, what counts as evidence against it, and how it responds to anomaly — depends essentially on whether its foundational claims are a priori or a posteriori. Systems of the two kinds should not be treated as interchangeable formalizations of the same underlying physical content; they encode different views of what a law of motion *is*, and that difference shows up in their logical behavior, not merely in their philosophical packaging.
