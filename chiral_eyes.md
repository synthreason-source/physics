This is really the "does everything match" question applied directly — and the answer depends entirely on whether the vitamin A and the rhodopsin are mirrored *to match each other* or not.

**Case 1: Both mirrored (mirror vitamin A + mirror rhodopsin) — matched system**

If the rhodopsin has been "chiralised" (mirrored) and the vitamin A supplied is also the mirror-image enantiomer, they should fit together just as well as the normal pair does:

- Mirror vitamin A would be converted to mirror 11-cis-retinal by the corresponding mirror-image enzymes (mirror RPE65, mirror retinol dehydrogenase) — assuming those are present too, since enzyme conversion is itself chirality-dependent.
- Mirror 11-cis-retinal would bind into the mirror rhodopsin's binding pocket correctly, since the pocket geometry and the chromophore geometry are complementary mirror images of each other — shape-matching works the same regardless of which "hand" the whole system is.
- Light absorption and the cis→trans photoisomerization trigger would work identically, since (as discussed before) ordinary light itself is achiral and doesn't care about the molecule's handedness.
- The downstream signaling cascade would also need to be all-mirror (transducin, PDE, etc.) to actually transmit the signal, but assuming that's also consistent, the whole visual cycle should function normally.

**Case 2: Mismatched (normal vitamin A + mirrored rhodopsin, or vice versa)**

This is where it breaks down:
- Normal (natural) vitamin A processed by mirror-image enzymes wouldn't fit their active sites correctly — poor or no conversion to retinal at all.
- Even if somehow converted, normal-chirality retinal likely wouldn't fit properly into a mirror-image rhodopsin binding pocket — same "wrong-handed glove" mismatch problem as before.
- Net effect: little to no functional visual pigment forms. This would look like a functional vitamin A deficiency specifically localized to that mirrored tissue — the mirror rhodopsin would essentially be "starved" of a chromophore it can actually use, even though vitamin A is technically present in the system.

**The key principle**

This example is a clean illustration of something chirality chemists emphasize: chirality-matching is *relative*, not absolute. A mirror rhodopsin isn't defective — it's just as functional as normal rhodopsin, but only when paired with mirror-matched partners all the way down the chain (mirror retinal, mirror transducin, mirror everything). Feed it a normal-chirality nutrient, and it's the equivalent of trying to plug a device into the wrong-shaped socket — not broken, just incompatible.
