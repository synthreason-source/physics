

# Linearised Threat-Processing Hypothesis

The **linearised threat-processing hypothesis** is a proposed theoretical framework suggesting that the fight-or-flight response operates as a sequential, bottom-up computational process rather than a unitary, fully integrated reflex. The hypothesis draws an analogy between threat appraisal and the step-wise, state-based operation of a Turing machine, proposing that this architecture may account for inconsistencies — or "anomalies" — observed in real-world fight-or-flight responses.

This is a speculative framework rather than an established model in mainstream neuroscience or evolutionary psychology, and it has not been formally tested or peer-reviewed under this name.

## Overview

Conventional accounts treat the fight-or-flight response as an evolved, largely automatic reaction to perceived danger, mediated chiefly by the amygdala and the sympathetic nervous system. The linearised threat-processing hypothesis reframes this system as a **discrete, sequential computation**: sensory input is processed in ordered steps, similar to how a Turing machine reads and acts on symbols one at a time, rather than through continuous, parallel integration with other cognitive systems.

## Core concepts

### Bottom-up, linear processing
The hypothesis holds that threat detection proceeds in a fixed, bottom-up order — from raw sensory input toward a behavioral output — with limited or no influence from higher-order (top-down) reasoning at the earliest stages. This contrasts with models in which threat appraisal is shaped concurrently by context, memory, and conscious evaluation.

### The divisor function
A central feature of the model is a proposed **divisor function**: a categorical sorting mechanism, plausibly localized to structures such as the amygdala, that partitions incoming stimuli into discrete classes (e.g., "threat" versus "non-threat") before any further evaluation occurs. This is presented as a classification step rather than a literal arithmetic operation.

### Non-interstitial channel
The hypothesis describes the threat-response pathway as **non-interstitial** — structurally insulated from, rather than blended with, other ongoing cognitive processes. Because the pathway does not "intersect" with concurrent systems such as deliberative reasoning, its output can proceed independently of — and sometimes in conflict with — contextually appropriate judgment.

### Turing machine analogy
The comparison to a Turing machine is used to characterize the threat-response system as operating over discrete states with a defined sequence of transitions, rather than as a continuous or holistic process. The analogy is offered as a heuristic model of computational structure, not a claim of literal mechanistic equivalence.

## Proposed explanatory significance

Proponents of the framework suggest that treating threat response as an isolated, sequential computation — rather than an integrated one — may explain why fight-or-flight reactions sometimes appear disproportionate, delayed, or mismatched to context ("anomalies"). Under this view, the response system still computes "correctly" relative to its own inputs, but its insulation from other cognitive systems can produce outputs poorly suited to non-ancestral or socially complex environments.

## Status and criticism

As of this writing, the hypothesis has not been formally published, tested, or evaluated in peer-reviewed literature. It draws on established neuroscience (e.g., the amygdala's role in threat detection) and computational theory (the Turing machine model) as metaphors rather than as directly validated mechanisms. Critics of similar speculative models often note that:
- The brain's threat-response circuitry (amygdala, hypothalamus, periaqueductal gray, etc.) shows extensive bidirectional and parallel connectivity with cortical regions, which may be difficult to reconcile with a strictly linear, non-interstitial model.
- Analogies to discrete computational systems like Turing machines are common heuristic devices in cognitive science but are rarely treated as literal architectural claims without supporting empirical or computational modeling.
