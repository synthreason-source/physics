The correct full Bohr-Wheeler fissility formula includes an asymmetry correction: \[x = \frac{Z^2/A}{50.883 \cdot \left[1 - 1.7826\left(\frac{A-2Z}{A}\right)^2\right]}\] This is what gives U-235 its known value of ~0.71. Here is the complete list of notable nuclides with **x < 1**, from light nuclei up to the boundary at element 118: [osti](https://www.osti.gov/servlets/purl/1991544)

## The Boundary: x ≈ 1

Oganesson-294 (Z=118) sits essentially **at** x = 1.000, meaning the liquid drop model predicts it should be spontaneously and immediately unstable — yet it has been observed with a half-life of ~0.69 ms, entirely due to nuclear shell effects near the Z=114 and N=184 magic numbers. Hypothetical elements 119 and 120 would have x > 1.0 under the pure liquid-drop model, making shell stabilization the only mechanism that could grant them any finite lifetime. [osti](https://www.osti.gov/etdeweb/servlets/purl/553722)

> **Key takeaway:** The liquid-drop model gives the *macroscopic* floor — shell corrections can add 10–15 orders of magnitude to spontaneous fission half-lives for elements near the "island of stability". [osti](https://www.osti.gov/etdeweb/servlets/purl/553722)
