
# The transverse electric field is the Coulomb repulsion between the two halves of a deforming nucleus. The Bohr-Wheeler liquid drop model quantifies instability via the fissility parameter:

x=Z2A
Where:
Z = atomic number (proton count)
A = mass number (total nucleons)
When x≥1, Coulomb energy exceeds surface tension energy and the nucleus is spontaneously unstable against fission. Uranium-235 has x≈0.71, requiring a neutron to push it over the barrier.

give me a list of x<1

The correct full Bohr-Wheeler fissility formula includes an asymmetry correction: $$
x = \frac{Z^2/A}{50.883 \cdot \left[1 - 1.7826\left(\frac{A-2Z}{A}\right)^2\right]}
$$ This is what gives U-235 its known value of ~0.71. Here is the complete list of notable nuclides with **x < 1**, from light nuclei up to the boundary at element 118:[^1]

## The Boundary: x ≈ 1

Oganesson-294 (Z=118) sits essentially **at** x = 1.000, meaning the liquid drop model predicts it should be spontaneously and immediately unstable — yet it has been observed with a half-life of ~0.69 ms, entirely due to nuclear shell effects near the Z=114 and N=184 magic numbers. Hypothetical elements 119 and 120 would have x > 1.0 under the pure liquid-drop model, making shell stabilization the only mechanism that could grant them any finite lifetime.[^5][^6][^4]

> **Key takeaway:** The liquid-drop model gives the *macroscopic* floor — shell corrections can add 10–15 orders of magnitude to spontaneous fission half-lives for elements near the "island of stability".[^5]
