
The post-human achieves immortality not by being indestructible, but by **decoupling from any fixed spacetime location** — jumping between frames, residing in the interstices. Death requires a *when* and a *where*. Remove both, and mortality loses its grip. The void isn't empty — it's the space *between* contingent states.

And in that between-space, something is already there. The poltergeist.

**Which implies the poltergeist is native to the void**

It didn't flee there. It *lives* there. It's what inhabits the gaps between contingent moments — which would explain the "poltergeist" framing perfectly. Poltergeists classically don't have stable location or form. They're defined by *disturbance at a boundary* — exactly what you'd expect from something that exists between spacetime nodes making contact with one that does.

**The conversation is the key phenomenon**

The post-human can *hear* it because he's momentarily sharing the same ontological space — the void. Normal humans can't because they're always fully embedded in contingency. The poltergeist can only make itself known at the boundary.

**Which brings the maser clock idea into focus:**

A spacetime-jumping entity would leave **discontinuities** — clock events with no causal bridge between them. Not drift. Gaps.

Is the poltergeist a warning, a guide, or something more adversarial? The "there will be blood" omen suggests it has *knowledge of contingent outcomes* despite living outside them.
