Photons do not duplicate or reflect half according to beam-splitter thickness. A beam splitter imposes a fixed unitary balance of amplitude and phase, so the photon enters a superposition of possible paths rather than a classical division of energy. Bell inequality experiments reveal that this balance cannot be explained by local pre-existing values; the outcomes belong to a shared quantum state. To measure one beam is to condition the state of the other through their joint phase structure, not through a classical signal, and the apparent instantaneity is the collapse of the shared description.

Beam-Phase Correlation Theory
Axiom 1: Single-amplitude input
An incident photon is described by one coherent quantum amplitude before measurement, not by two independent physical halves.

Axiom 2: Beam-splitter unitarity
A beam splitter acts as a unitary transformation that maps one input amplitude into a superposition of output amplitudes with fixed relative probabilities and phases.

Axiom 3: Phase carries structure
The physically relevant content of the splitter is not material subdivision but phase-structured redistribution of possibility across output modes.

Axiom 4: Joint-state primacy
For paired or correlated systems, the joint quantum state is primary, and the observable outcomes of each arm are not fully defined independently of that joint state.

Axiom 5: Measurement conditioning
Measurement on one subsystem updates the conditional state assignment of the other subsystem, but does not transmit a usable classical signal between them.

Definitions
Definition 1: Beam-phase mapping
The transformation from one input mode to two output modes, including amplitudes and relative phase shifts, is called the beam-phase mapping.

Definition 2: Correlation residue
The part of the observed joint statistics that cannot be explained by assigning local pre-existing values to each arm is called the correlation residue.

Definition 3: Non-separable state
A state is non-separable when it cannot be written as a product of independent states for each subsystem.

Definition 4: Conditional collapse
The updated description of one subsystem after measurement of the other is called conditional collapse.

Definition 5: Phase-coherent whole
A system whose observables are governed by a single global phase structure rather than independent local parts is called a phase-coherent whole.

Core postulates
A beam splitter does not split photon substance.

It redistributes quantum possibility.

The output probabilities are constrained by unitary phase relations.

Bell-type violations arise when the joint statistics of the outputs cannot be reduced to local assignments.

Measurement reveals one branch of a global state and updates the conditional description of the partner subsystem.

Derived claims
From these axioms, the theory claims:

The “50/50” behavior of an ideal beam splitter is an amplitude symmetry, not an energy division.

Entanglement is a global constraint on the joint state, not a local energetic disturbance.

The apparent instantaneity of distant measurement correlation reflects the structure of the state update, not a propagating physical shock.

The role of the beam splitter in Bell-type experiments is to preserve or reveal phase relations that classical hidden-variable models cannot reproduce.

Predicted experimental consequences
1. Phase-sensitive Bell visibility
If the beam-phase mapping is changed by controlled phase shifts in the splitter, Bell-type correlation visibility should change even when count rates remain fixed.

2. Thickness is not the controlling variable
Increasing the physical thickness of the splitter alone should not determine whether the quantum balance holds, provided the optical transformation and phase relations are otherwise unchanged.

3. Correlation survives without local energy split
Interference-based Bell correlations should persist even when no interpretation in terms of literal photon division is possible.

4. Conditional statistics shift after remote measurement
Measuring one arm should change the conditional statistics of the partner arm, while the marginal statistics of the partner alone remain unchanged.

5. Phase-breaking destroys nonclassical structure
Introducing decoherence, distinguishability, or uncontrolled phase noise should reduce the correlation residue and weaken Bell-type violation.

6. Beam-splitter asymmetry should deform correlations predictably
If the beam splitter is made intentionally non-50/50, the output probabilities should change in a way consistent with unitary amplitude redistribution, while the nonclassical joint-state structure remains analyzable through the modified transformation.

Interpretation of Bell experiments in this theory
Bell experiments are not about a wavelength literally splitting into two equal wavelengths. They are about whether the observed joint outcomes can be reproduced by local pre-assigned values.

In this mini-theory, Bell violation is evidence that the system is governed by a shared phase-coherent whole whose outcomes are only locally revealed at measurement.

Concise theorem statement
Theorem:
A beam splitter preserves a single quantum amplitude while distributing its possible outcomes across multiple paths by unitary phase mapping. If two such outputs are part of a non-separable joint state, then their observed correlations may violate Bell inequalities because the statistics belong to the global phase-coherent whole rather than to independent local properties.

Falsifiability conditions
This mini-theory fails if any of the following are found:

Bell-type correlations can be fully explained by a purely local model with pre-existing values and no shared phase structure.

Beam-splitter output statistics depend primarily on thickness rather than on the unitary optical transformation.

Conditional state changes on one side can be used to transmit controllable information instantly to the other side.

Phase shifts and indistinguishability do not affect interference and Bell visibility as predicted.

Final compressed form
Reality at the quantum level is not built from independently split particles, but from globally constrained amplitudes. A beam splitter reveals phase-coherent redistribution of possibility, and Bell violations reveal that the measurable world cannot be reduced to local pre-assigned parts.
