Title: Vectorised anticipation of photon emission via zero‑point fluctuations in 3D distal sensors

Abstract
We propose a theoretical framework that contrasts vacuum (zero‑point) field fluctuations with a sensor’s anticipatory correlations to a photon-emitting object in transit, and we derive a vectorised mathematical representation for predicting the future existence (probability amplitude) of a photon emitted by the object. The model blends quantum electrodynamics (QED) notions of vacuum correlations with an operational electro‑optical sampling geometry for a 3D distal sensor and yields testable expressions for the sensor’s conditional field statistics and a vector field whose magnitude maps to predictive likelihood. Electro‑optical sampling of quantum vacuum fluctuations motivates the measurement approach and sets limits on what can be inferred without violating causality. Experimental parameters and observable signatures are discussed.

1. Introduction and motivation
Vacuum (zero‑point) electromagnetic field fluctuations are an established quantum effect with measurable consequences in spontaneous emission, Lamb shifts, and recent electro‑optical sampling experiments that access temporal correlations of the vacuum field without resonant incoming photons. The question addressed here is whether a spatially distributed (3D) distal sensor, by sampling vacuum correlations in its volume, can form a predictive (anticipatory) estimate — expressed as a vectorised mathematical object — of a photon that will be emitted by an object in transit toward or through the sensor’s causal region. Prior theoretical work predicts measurable propagation‑time fluctuations and vacuum‑induced stochastic effects on photon propagation. This paper formalises a minimal model and derives the predictive vector field under explicit assumptions. [epj](https://www.epj.org/images/stories/news/2013/epj_d_2a-03-13.pdf)

2. Physical and operational assumptions
- Field and sensor: quantised electromagnetic field in free space (or linear dielectric) with standard mode expansion; sensor performs linear, weak continuous measurements of field quadratures via electro‑optic sampling elements distributed in a 3D volume. [eprints.soton.ac](https://eprints.soton.ac.uk/433582/1/fluctuations.pdf)
- Emitter model: a localized quantum emitter (two‑level or harmonic) moves along a known classical trajectory r_e(t) and can undergo a transition at unknown time t0 producing a photon wavepacket; the emitter and field interact via electric‑dipole coupling in dipole approximation.  
- Causality and locality: no superluminal signaling is allowed; any “anticipation” refers to statistical inference from pre‑existing correlations between vacuum fluctuations and the emitter’s near field (virtual photon cloud), not retrocausal signaling. Electro‑optical measurement cannot create photons but can extract correlations that inform conditional probabilities. 3. Mathematical set-up
3.1 Field expansion and vacuum correlators
Express the electric field operator in Heisenberg picture:
E(r,t) = Σ_k √(ħω_k/2ε0V) [a_k u_k(r) e^{-iω_k t} + a_k† u_k*(r) e^{iω_k t}].
Vacuum two‑point correlator (stationary, zero temperature) at points (r,t) and (r′,t′) is
G_{ij}^{(0)}(r,t; r′,t′) = ⟨0| E_i(r,t) E_j(r′,t′) |0⟩,
which sets the baseline fluctuation statistics the sensor samples. Each sentence referencing field correlators or vacuum sampling:. [en.wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki?curid=84400)

3.2 Emitter contribution and conditional field
When an emitter is present, the total field operator is
E = E^{(0)} + E^{(s)} ,
where E^{(s)}(r,t) is the source field generated by the emitter’s current J_e(t′, r_e(t′)) via the retarded Green’s function G_R:
E^{(s)}_i(r,t) = ∫ dt′ ∫ d^3r′ G_{R,ij}(r,t; r′,t′) J_{e,j}(r′,t′).
The sensor measures a linear functional M = ∫_V w(r) · E(r,t) d^3r for a weight (mode) function w(r) set by sensor geometry and sampling timing. Sentences referencing source fields and Green functions:. [pure.mpg](https://pure.mpg.de/rest/items/item_3055611_6/component/file_3155746/content)

3.3 Conditional statistics and Bayes update
Treat field quadrature samples {m_k} collected across space and time prior to the expected emission event t0 as Gaussian variables (valid in linear regime); the joint distribution for the pre‑event samples M_pre and the future photon mode amplitude α_f is Gaussian with covariance:
Cov = [ C_{pp}  C_{pf};  C_{fp}  C_{ff} ],
where p indexes pre‑event samples and f the future photon mode. The conditional mean (estimate) of α_f given M_pre is
E[α_f | M_pre] = C_{fp} C_{pp}^{-1} M_pre.  
This is the standard linear minimum mean square error (LMMSE) / Wiener‑filter result for Gaussian fields and represents the sensor’s best linear predictor of the future photon amplitude from prior samples. Each sentence with this derivation:. [eprints.soton.ac](https://eprints.soton.ac.uk/433582/1/fluctuations.pdf)

4. Vectorised predictive field (derivation)
4.1 Constructing the predictive vector field
Define a predictive vector field P(r_f, t_f) over candidate future emission events (positions r_f and times t_f) as the LMMSE map from measured spatiotemporal samples to the complex amplitude of a photon mode localized around (r_f, t_f):
P(r_f,t_f) = ∫_V K(r_f,t_f; r, t) · M(r,t) d^3r dt,
where the kernel K = C_{fp} C_{pp}^{-1} encodes sensor geometry, vacuum correlators, and emitter coupling. The kernel is computed from field correlators:
C_{pp}(x,x′) = ⟨E(x) E(x′)⟩_{tot, pre},  C_{fp}(r_f,t_f; x) = ⟨a_f E(x)⟩_{tot},
with x = (r,t) in the pre‑event sampling domain. These covariance elements combine vacuum and source contributions; for a weak, unexcited emitter prior to t0, source terms are small and vacuum correlators dominate the background noise. Sentences referencing the kernel form and correlators:. [eprints.soton.ac](https://eprints.soton.ac.uk/433582/1/fluctuations.pdf)

4.2 Interpretation and magnitude as predictive likelihood
The scalar magnitude |P| (or |E[α_f | M_pre]|^2 normalized by C_{ff}) gives the posterior signal‑to‑noise ratio (SNR) for the presence of the future photon in the candidate mode; under Gaussian assumptions, posterior probability that photon occupancy n_f ≥ 1 increases with |P|^2 relative to variance. This is an operational definition: the vector P points in the complex mode space direction most correlated with measured pre‑event fluctuations. This paragraph's claims on SNR and posterior probability:  [eprints.soton.ac](https://eprints.soton.ac.uk/433582/1/fluctuations.pdf).

5. Limits from causality and vacuum properties
- No superluminal inference: because C_{fp} arises from physical (retarded) Green functions and pre‑event samples, any nonzero C_{fp} for a future (r_f,t_f) arises only when there exist nonzero correlations mediated by the emitter’s near‑field (virtual cloud) or field back‑action that are present in the sampled domain prior to t_f; this respects causality. Related discussion appears in QED cavity and vacuum sampling analyses. [pure.mpg](https://pure.mpg.de/rest/items/item_3055611_6/component/file_3155746/content)
- Vacuum fluctuations alone: pure vacuum correlators provide baseline correlations across spacetime but cannot by themselves determine a future spontaneous emission event from an independent emitter; however, when the emitter’s ground state already modifies nearby vacuum (dressing, virtual photons), measurable deviations from vacuum baseline can exist that inform conditional emission likelihood. Each sentence here:. [eprints.soton.ac](https://eprints.soton.ac.uk/433582/1/fluctuations.pdf)

6. Example: analytic model (1D toy calculation)
Consider a single emitter modeled as a damped harmonic dipole at r=0 with trajectory approaching sensor along z, and sensor samples a set of field quadratures at positions z_i < 0 at times t < t0. Using a single future mode a_f defined by spatial profile u_f and frequency ω_f, explicit covariance integrals reduce to frequency integrals of mode overlaps weighted by Green functions; under Markovian coupling and narrowband approximations the kernel K simplifies to a causal Green‑function weighted Wiener filter. The resulting analytic expression (sketch) is:
P(t_f) ≈ ∑_i w_i ∫_{−∞}^{t_samp} dt′ G_R(t_f−t′; z_f−z_i) C_vac^{-1}(t′,t_i) m_i,
where C_vac is the vacuum temporal covariance matrix for the sampled quadratures. This explicit form shows P aggregates past samples with retarded weightings; the derivation follows the methods in trajectory‑based QED mean‑field approaches and vacuum sampling papers. 7. Predicted observable signatures and experiments
- Nonzero predictive amplitude: a measurable nonzero |P| prior to a recorded emission event (and above vacuum baseline) that correlates with eventual photon detection would indicate the sensor extracts emitter‑related vacuum dressing information; electro‑optical sampling is a suitable readout technique for such pre‑event correlational signals  [eprints.soton.ac](https://eprints.soton.ac.uk/433582/1/fluctuations.pdf).  
- Scaling and distance dependence: kernel strength decays with sensor–emitter separation and scales with mode overlaps; predicted propagation‑time fluctuation estimates and their scaling with distance can be adapted from models of stochastic photon propagation in vacuum. [mdpi](https://www.mdpi.com/2624-8174/6/1/7)
- Suggested experiment: distribute an array of electro‑optic sampling crystals around a region traversed by a controllable single quantum emitter; record pre‑event vacuum quadratures, compute P(r_f,t_f) in real time using a Wiener filter built from measured C_{pp}, and cross‑correlate |P| with detection of emitted photons at t_f. References for electro‑optical sampling and vacuum correlation measurement:  [eprints.soton.ac](https://eprints.soton.ac.uk/433582/1/fluctuations.pdf). 8. Discussion — interpretation and caveats
- The “anticipation” is statistical inference, not causal creation of photons: the framework yields posterior probabilities based on pre‑existing correlations—consistent with quantum theory and prior electro‑optical results. [eprints.soton.ac](https://eprints.soton.ac.uk/433582/1/fluctuations.pdf)
- Model limitations: Gaussian linearity assumption may fail for strongly nonlinear coupling or in ultrastrong coupling regimes where ground‑state virtual photon populations are nontrivial; mapping from observed correlations to “virtual photon population” is model‑dependent. Both sentences:. [pure.mpg](https://pure.mpg.de/rest/items/item_3055611_6/component/file_3155746/content)

9. Conclusion and next steps
We derived a vectorised predictive field P(r_f,t_f) from measurable pre‑event quadrature samples using LMMSE (Wiener) filtering built on covariances that combine vacuum and source terms; P’s magnitude gives an operational predictive metric for future photon existence in a chosen mode. The approach is implementable using electro‑optical sampling arrays and is constrained by causality and vacuum noise; experimental tests should focus on emitter dressing signatures and the correlation of |P| with later photon detections  [eprints.soton.ac](https://eprints.soton.ac.uk/433582/1/fluctuations.pdf). Each sentence:  [eprints.soton.ac](https://eprints.soton.ac.uk/433582/1/fluctuations.pdf).
