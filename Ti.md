This paper proposes a speculative framework linking subjective temporal orientation to structural properties of physical systems. We introduce the temporal index as a conceptual parameter describing whether an agent's model of its own experience is oriented toward retrospection (negative index) or anticipation (positive index), and ask what would follow, formally, if this orientation were treated as a variable coupled to the agent's physical description rather than as a purely phenomenological one. Under this proposal, a negative temporal index is associated with an increase in effective mass-density terms in the agent's local description — a metaphor for how retrospective cognitive states might correlate with felt "weight" or inertia, without claiming a mechanism in fundamental physics. We further explore a heuristic construct, the topological energy membrane, as a boundary structure separating an agent's internal state space from its environment, within which localized "quanta" represent discrete units of decision-relevant information. We outline, at a conceptual level, how such membranes might be "constructed" by treating decisions as boundary-defining operations that precede — rather than follow — the physical rule-set governing a system's evolution, inverting the usual causal order of state-then-decision. We emphasize throughout that this is a conceptual and philosophical exercise in formal analogy-building, not a physical theory with predictive or empirical content, and we close by identifying what would be required (operational definitions, falsifiable predictions, dimensional consistency) to move any of these constructs toward testability.
